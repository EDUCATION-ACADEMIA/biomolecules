Which structure in a nucelotide is different among the 5 different nucleotides?

(A) R-Groups

(B) Phosphate Group

(C) Sugar

(D) ** Nitrogenous Base

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The five-carbon sugar ring and the content of the nitrogenous base between DNA and RNA are slightly different from each other. Four different types of nitrogenous bases are found in DNA: adenine (A), thymine (T), cytosine (C), and guanine (G). In RNA, the thymine is replaced by uracil (U).

Concept note-2: -The adenosine phosphate nucleotides have ribose as the five carbon sugar and adenine as the nitrogenous base. These nucleotides differ in the number of inorganic phosphate groups phosphorylated to the ribose, which can vary from one to three.

Concept note-3: -In RNA strands, thymine is replaced by uracil. Also, the sugar part of the nucleotides in RNA is different than the one in DNA, having one extra oxygen.

Concept note-4: -The five bases that are found in nucleotides are often represented by their initial letter: adenine, A; guanine, G; cytosine, C; thymine, T; and uracil, U. Note that A, G, C and T occur in DNA; A, G, C and U occur in RNA.

Concept note-5: -A nucleotide is composed of a nitrogenous base, deoxyribose (five-carbon sugar), and at least one phosphate group. The nitrogenous bases are purines such as adenine (A) and guanine (G), or pyrimidines such as cytosine (C), thymine (T), and uracil (U).
