Can lipids dissolve in water? Why or why not?

(A) ** No, because they are non-polar

(B) Yes, because they are polar

(C) No, because they are polar

(D) Yes, because they are non-polar

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -They are water insoluble. Lipids are non-polar molecules while water is polar molecule, so there is no way for water to attach to a lipid molecule. Instead, the non-polar lipids will bind to themselves, which is why oils and fats form droplets on the surface of water.

Concept note-2: -Lipids are chemical substances that are nonpolar in nature. Because water is a polar molecule, they are only soluble in nonpolar solvents and insoluble in water.

Concept note-3: -Non-polar molecules do not dissolve well in polar solutions like water; in fact, polar and non-polar molecules tend to repel each other in the same way that oil and water donâ€™t mix and will separate from each other even if they are shaken vigorously in an attempt to mix them.

Concept note-4: -Lipids are all insoluble in polar solvents like water but highly soluble in the non-polar or weakly polar organic solvents, including ether, chloroform, benzene, and acetone.
